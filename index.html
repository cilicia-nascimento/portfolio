Profile

Originally from Brazil, I built the foundation of my scientific career there before moving to the United States in 2025. My path has always run in parallel between research and teaching: alongside my master's and PhD training in microbiology and molecular biology, I mentored junior students and contributed to course instruction alongside my Principal Investigator.

I haven't yet led an independent course as primary instructor, but I've accumulated substantial guest-lecturing experience in both Brazil and the United States, and I've built a deliberate practice of observing, reflecting on, and refining how I teach — most recently through Baylor College of Medicine's Biomedical Educator Certificate and the ACTOR (Acquiring Competencies in Teaching through Observation & Reflection) program.

My earlier international experience — a Fulbright-sponsored year in Rhode Island (2012) and a TOP China scholarship month in Shanghai (2014) — shaped an early, lasting commitment to education that has stayed central through every stage of my research career.

Teaching Philosophy

As an educator, I am deeply committed to fostering an inclusive, engaging, and dynamic learning environment where all students feel valued, respected, and empowered — a space where every student is welcomed and encouraged to bring their full self into the learning experience.

Inclusive & Empowering

"Every student—regardless of racial, religious, ethnic, gender, or socioeconomic background—is welcomed and encouraged to bring their full self into the learning experience."

Curiosity Over Completion

"Learning is a lifelong journey, not a destination... Education should ignite curiosity, inspire critical thinking, and encourage discovery."

Student-Centered & Flexible

"No two students learn in the exact same way" — met with experiential learning, project-based work, discussion, and technology-enhanced instruction.

Collaborative by Nature

"Science is collaborative by nature" — students build a supportive community that challenges and inspires each other, and helps shape the course itself.

Passion & command of subject

The most common word in open-ended feedback: students repeatedly cite confidence and enthusiasm as what made sessions land — "her passion and expertise," "clearly has a very well understanding of the subject," "knew what they were talking about so it was easier to follow along."

Relatable examples

Pop-culture references, games, and visuals came up often as engagement tools — students specifically called out anime and TV-show comparisons, colorful diagrams, and real-world examples as what kept them following along outside the material itself.

Pacing

The most frequent piece of constructive feedback: several students asked for a slightly slower pace, while others specifically praised the pacing as "not too fast, not too slow" — suggesting it varies by session and is a known, addressable lever.

Reading the pattern: comfort asking questions (72%) is the lowest-scoring dimension, and pacing is the top open-ended comment — together they point to the same thing: students who are still processing fast-moving material are less likely to speak up. Building in explicit pause points is a small change that plausibly moves both numbers.

A Course I Built: Microbiology 101

Introduction to Microbiology
A full-semester undergraduate course exploring the micro-world — designed, syllabus and all, from unit structure to grading philosophy to individual lesson plans.
5 units 15-week calendar Lab + lecture No science background required
Who's teaching it: Cilicia introduces herself to students as a São Paulo native "used to a chaotic life," who fell for microbiology the same way she hopes her students will — through one undergraduate course that made her think, "this is awesome, I need to know more!" Office hours come with an open invitation to talk hobbies, including her current one: crochet.
Unit 1
A Microbe Introduction
History of microbiology, microscopy fundamentals, and bacterial cell structure.
Unit 2
Bacterial Functions & Identification
Genetics, metabolism, DNA technology, and hands-on biochemical identification in the lab.
Unit 3
Meet the Microorganisms
Gram-positive/negative bacteria, microbial disease, the human microbiome, and epidemiology.
Unit 4
Applying the Knowledge
System-by-system human infections, taught in part by student groups presenting their own research.
Unit 5
Broadening Our Horizons
Virology, mycology, and environmental microbiology to close out the semester.

"Unit Science Time!"

Every unit closes by reading and discussing a current primary-literature article together — treating scientific reading as a practiced skill, not an afterthought.

Choose Your Weights

Students pick how their final grade is distributed across activities, quizzes, and the final assignment from four preset weighting options.

Peer-Taught Units

Small groups research a chosen pathogen and teach it to the rest of the class — then grade each other's presentations using a shared rubric.

Assignment, Your Way

The final assignment can take the form of an essay, presentation, video, lecture, or quiz — whatever best shows what a student learned.
